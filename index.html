Proposition

Allegations of police misconduct disproportionately target younger officers, indicating potential biases or issues in training and supervision.

1. Scatter Plot Visualization Assessment

Design Choice Earnest / Deceptive Score Rationale
Scatter plot of raw counts vs. age -1 Uses raw allegation counts without adjusting for the number of officers at each age, possibly exaggerating trends tied to larger age-group populations.
Linear regression trendline -0.5 Suggests a smooth, strong negative relationship between age and allegations, even though the underlying relationship may not be linear.

2. Bar Chart Visualization Assessment

Design Choice Earnest / Deceptive Score Rationale
Normalization by age group size +1.5 Provides a fair comparison across age groups by controlling for how many officers are in each group. Enables proportionate interpretation of allegations.
Uniform single-color bars (blue) +1.5 Reduces perceptual bias by removing visual emphasis. Encourages interpretation based solely on data differences rather than styling.
Simple, neutral title and labeling +1 Frames the visualization in a factual tone, suggesting objectivity and limiting rhetorical influence.

Final Reflection

The process of designing the scatter and regression chart, and the bar chart really showed how small choices can dramatically shift a story. On one hand, binning ages and normalizing allegation counts felt straightforward: once the data were cleaned, it was a matter of grouping and division. On the other hand, deciding how to color and annotate those plots revealed itself as the trickiest part. Even though the underlying numbers didn’t change, tweaking marker opacity or choosing a single hue versus a gradient completely altered what draws your eye first. I was surprised by how easily a single arrow or a bold regression line could overpower the data’s true ambiguity.

This experience has refined my definition of “ethical analysis and visualization” into a balance between persuasive clarity and transparent disclosure. Ethical visuals should never force a conclusion the data don’t support; instead, they should guide the viewer toward the patterns the analyst genuinely believes are there. That means declaring every normalization step, explaining why outliers were deemphasized, and annotating confidence intervals honestly. In practice, ethical visualization means wielding design tools, color, scale, and annotation, only to illuminate, not to obscure.

What is the line between acceptable persuasion and outright deception? Acceptable persuasion uses visual emphasis to spotlight what is already a statistically credible trend. Misleading choices, by contrast, actively distort the data: truncating axes to exaggerate tiny changes, aggregating categories until all nuance vanishes, or coloring points to divert attention from inconvenient clusters. If any design decision hides a data transformation or manipulates perception without clear disclosure, it slips into the realm of deception. In my view, that boundary is bright.
